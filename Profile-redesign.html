Dream11 · Fan Identity System

The Missing Identity Layer

Dream11 is India's biggest fantasy sports platform. 220 million users watch cricket, football, and F1 together. They had no way to show who they are — or find each other. That was the gap.

This is the story of building an identity layer from scratch. Not a profile redesign. The infrastructure that makes social features possible.

The profile is a side effect. The real output is structured fandom data: team, players, sport. That's what everything else runs on.

Problem

01 — The Real Problem

Watch Party had no idea who you were

Watch Party is built for sports tribes. Shared viewing, real-time chat, creator-led rooms. It had all of it. It was missing the one thing that makes social products actually work: a way to know who's in the room with you.

You could sit next to a lifelong MI fan in a match room and have no idea. No signal. No tribe. No reason to find them again. The features weren't wrong. The foundation was missing.

Design

03 — The Sharp Wedge

One moment. One data point. Everything else is downstream.

Team pick. That's all you need. The cover, the jerseys, the personalised feed — all of it runs on that single declaration.

The core question
How do you get 220 million users to tell you who they support — at the exact moment they're most motivated to do it?

The motivation already exists. The window is narrow. The answer: don't ask directly.

The trick: embed the declaration inside something users already want
We put team selection inside the AI avatar flow. When a user picks Ferrari, they think they're customising their avatar. They're making the product's most important data declaration.

Visual Exploration

05 — Visual Exploration

Four directions. Two not taken forward. One that evolved.

Not all of these failed the same way. Two were wrong from the start. One was right in concept and wrong in execution — and that distinction matters. That's where the real thinking happened.

Direction 1 — The Locker Room Rejected

Strong visual. Wrong metaphor.
The strongest visual in the exploration — kit, helmet, bat. I killed it. A locker room is where players go, not fans. No amount of polish fixes a wrong metaphor. Hardest cut of the project.

Direction 2 — Sports Memorabilia Rejected

Right idea. Outdated medium.
Glass cases, spotlit jerseys, pegboard layouts. The memorabilia metaphor translated faithfully to screen — that was the problem. Fans don't want a museum. They want to be the hero.

Direction 3 — Digital Wall Evolved → Final

First direction that centred the fan.
Fan as hero. Jerseys as collectibles. Finally felt right. Then the math: unique assets per team × per player × per sport = a pipeline nobody can maintain. The concept was correct. The production model was impossible.

This is where the model clicked: identity as collectible signals — not as asset-heavy UI. That insight survived. The execution didn't.

Direction 4 — The Fan Card Selected

Takes the core idea. Removes the complexity.
Bold team colour. AI avatar as the hero. Favourite players as numbered jerseys, favourite teams as flag tiles. One compositing system. 180+ teams. No unique assets per team. Swap the HSL value — the whole card changes. That's the system.
Building the System

06 — Building the System

How it works at scale

Cover card — one architecture. 180+ teams.

The naive version: one background image per team. 180+ assets to design, maintain, and update every time a team rebrands. The right version: one compositing system. Base HSL → SVG pattern → AI avatar → gradient → footer. Swap the HSL. The cover changes.

HSL colour mapping — 180+ teams, delivered to engineering via Claude Code.

Cover variations — one architecture across all teams.

Jersey system — one PNG. Infinite variations.

First instinct: write a Python script, generate a unique PNG per player per team. I built it. Then did the math.

  • 1

07 — Scoping for IPL

IPL moved the deadline. Here's what actually shipped.

We needed to be live before IPL. Most cuts were straightforward. One wasn't.

The moderation trade-off
Two moderation checkpoints were meant to sit at separate points in the flow. Engineering consolidated them — running two sequential APIs made an already-slow generation time worse. One error state now covers both failure modes. "This photo didn't pass our guidelines" is slightly less specific than I wanted. Trade-off accepted.

08 — AI in the Process

AI helped reduce dependency and speed up execution.

Not a flex. A working pattern for moving faster without adding headcount to every decision.

Problem framing
ChatGPT Claude
Used LLMs to structure the identity system and define key metrics before design began. Reduced early back-and-forth with product and engineering on scope.
Output: shared framing doc before Figma was opened
Prototyping
Google AI Studio
Built a working fan-avatar prototype to validate visual feasibility before engineering committed a sprint. Stakeholders reacted to something real, not a description.
Output: live prototype for go/no-go alignment
Production support
Claude Code
Generated HSL colour mappings for 180+ teams and wrote scripts for the asset pipeline. Delivered directly to engineering as usable assets — not reference files.
Output: production-ready assets, not mockups

AI Studio prototype — fan avatar generation, live

Before engineering committed a sprint, stakeholders saw this. A working prototype built in AI Studio — rough, real, and enough to get a yes.

Impact
Reduced back-and-forth with engineering on feasibility. Fewer alignment meetings because the prototype answered questions before they were asked.

Enabled faster iteration cycles. Problem framing upfront meant design decisions were grounded, not revisited.

Converted design decisions into production-ready assets. The HSL sheets and scripts went straight into the pipeline — no translation layer needed.

What I won't claim
I'm not drawing a line between "profile updated" and "more contest spend." Anyone doing that in a case study is making it up. The honest metrics: completion rate, avatar generation rate, and whether users who complete profiles behave differently in Watch Party. That data is 8–12 weeks post-IPL. Not sharing numbers I don't have.
Technical constraints I designed within
AI generation was hitting 10–15 seconds in testing — I designed a loading state buffer around that. Post-launch, scale changed the equation. Tech resolved the timing, but the first-frame quality issue was a moving target throughout. The real design problem: building a graceful experience around a pipeline that was still being optimised in parallel.
Takeaway

10 — The Takeaway

I didn't redesign a profile. I built the layer this product was missing.

The identity layer turns anonymous users into declared fans. That changes what the product can do.

01

Not just watching together — watching with people who support the same team.

None of these work without declared identity data. You can't personalise for a user you don't know.
