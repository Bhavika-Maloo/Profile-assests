01 — The Real Problem

Watch Party was a social product with no social substrate

Dream11's Watch Party is designed to create tribal, real-time sports experiences. The product has all the social mechanics — shared viewing, chat, reactions, creator-led rooms. But it was missing the one thing that makes social products compound over time: persistent identity.

Without identity, every Watch Party session resets. You don't know if the person next to you in chat is a lifelong MI fan or someone who clicked in by accident. There's no tribal signal, no basis for connection, no reason to find them again. The social loop was broken at the foundation — not because the features were wrong, but because there was no identity layer for them to build on.

The strategic framing
This isn't a profile redesign. It's building the identity layer that makes Watch Party's social mechanics actually function. A better-looking profile is a side effect. The real output is structured fandom data that enables personalisation, tribal nudges, and the network effects the product was designed around but couldn't yet deliver.

02 — The Strategic Bet

Identity enables a loop that doesn't exist yet

This was a bet on a future state, not a present one. The profile revamp wouldn't immediately fix Watch Party engagement — there's no clean causal line between "user updated profile" and "user spent more time in Watch Party." The bet was that building the identity layer now enables the personalization and social loops that create retention later.

That required being explicit with the team about what this work does and doesn't do. It builds infrastructure. It doesn't move a metric in the next sprint.

What this enables
Tribe-based WP recommendations. "X MI fans in this room" nudges. Rivalry detection. Fan-to-fan connection. Personalised content surfaces.
What this doesn't claim
Direct impact on contest entries, revenue, or D7 retention in Phase 1. Those are downstream effects that require the loop to compound first.
Why now
IPL was the highest-identity moment in the calendar. Users are already thinking about teams and players. The motivation to declare fandom peaks here.

03 — Mapping the Full System

Design the whole thing. Ship the wedge.

Before scoping down, I mapped the full identity system — a 7-stage progression from anonymous fan to community influencer. The purpose wasn't to ship all of it. It was to make sure every Phase 1 decision was made with a complete picture, not a local optimisation.

The complete 7-stage system. Stages 5–7 (orange) were future scope from day one — designed with full specs, deferred deliberately, not cut under pressure.

Why map beyond scope
Scope decisions made without a north star become arbitrary cuts. With the full map, every Phase 1 call had a clear rationale: we're not cutting badges, we're staging them. The tribal loyalty mechanic at Stage 4 isn't gone — it's waiting for the identity layer to have enough data to make it meaningful. That's a different conversation with a PM than "we ran out of time."

04 — The Sharp Wedge

One thing had to work above everything else

The profile has many components — cover card, jersey system, mini profile, nudges, completion bar. But if I'm honest about what this project hinged on, it was one thing: getting users to declare their fandom identity at the right moment.

Everything else is downstream of that. If a user never picks a team, the cover card is just a dark background. The jerseys are empty slots. The personalisation engine has nothing to work with. The entire system depends on that first declaration.

The wedge: fandom declaration embedded in avatar creation
We designed the team-selection step to live inside the AI avatar creation flow — not on a separate form, not as a standalone step. When a user uploads their photo and picks "Ferrari," they think they're choosing a colour scheme for their fan-pic. They're actually making the most important data declaration in the product. This wasn't an accident. It was the highest-motivation moment we had to collect that data — and we built the flow around it.

The avatar creation flow — team selection embedded at the moment of highest personal investment, not on a cold form

The face detection step

Lottie animation — "Detecting human face…"

Why this step exists
The face detection gate was a trust and safety requirement — users were uploading logos, deity images, and non-human content. But the loading state design was also doing a second job: it signals that something intelligent is happening. The Lottie animation makes the AI feel present, not invisible. Users who see "Detecting human face…" understand why the step exists, which reduces abandonment on what would otherwise feel like friction.

05 — Visual Exploration

Three directions rejected. One reason each.

The profile needed to feel like a sports fan's space — not a player's locker room, not a museum, not a reward shop. Each direction I explored failed for a specific reason. None of them were aesthetic failures.

Direction 1 — The Locker Room

Rejected — wrong person
The strongest visual direction in the entire exploration. Kit, helmet, bat — beautifully rendered. We spent real time here. Killed it because a locker room is where players go, not fans. Putting users in a locker room projects a player identity onto someone who watches from the stands. The metaphor was wrong at the root, and no amount of visual polish fixes a wrong metaphor. This was the hardest cut.

Direction 2 — Sports Memorabilia

Rejected — wrong era
Glass display cases, spotlit jerseys, pegboard layouts. The physical memorabilia metaphor translated faithfully to screen — which was the problem. It looked like a museum exhibit or a reward shop, not a 2026 mobile identity. Good concept, wrong medium. Fans in 2026 don't want a glass case — they want to be the hero.

Direction 3 — Digital Scroll / Pin Board

Rejected — not scalable at production
The visual direction finally clicked here — fan as the hero, jerseys as collectibles, a scrollable display of fandom. Loved it. Then did the math: unique assets per team × per player × per sport = a pipeline that doesn't exist and can't be maintained. The concept was right. The production model was impossible. Sometimes the right direction is the one you can actually build.

Direction 4 — The Digital Fan Card ✓

Chosen — fan-native, scalable, 2026
Bold team-coloured cover. AI avatar as the hero. G.O.A.T. list of players as jerseys. The metaphor finally fit: this is how a sports fan in 2026 would actually want to represent themselves. One structural problem surfaced immediately — teams and players were showing up in the same jersey grid. Same visual format, completely different meaning. That one observation became the most important IA decision in the project.

06 — Building the System

The decisions that made it work at scale

Cover card — one architecture, 180+ teams

The cover isn't a static image per team — that would be 180+ unique assets to maintain. It's a layered compositing system: base HSL colour from the team data → blend SVG pattern at ±12% variation for depth → AI avatar PNG → gradient → profile footer. Swap the HSL value, the whole cover changes.

Layer architecture (bottom → top): BG HSL → Blend SVG pattern → User image → Gradient → Profile footer → Active since tab → AI mark

The same system generating Argentina blue, Ferrari red, and Pakistan green — all from one compositing architecture

Claude Code — a production asset, not a design reference
To make the team-reactive cover work across 180+ teams, I used Claude Code to generate the complete HSL colour mapping data sheet across Cricket, Football, and F1. This wasn't busywork — it was a production asset that went directly to engineering. Nobody asked me to build it. It was just the right next question: if I design a system that responds to team colour, someone needs the colour data for every team. That someone was me.

Jersey system — one base PNG, infinite variations

First instinct: generate a unique PNG per player per team using a Python script. Built the script. Then stopped and did the math.

The wrong bet I caught before it became engineering's problem
Hundreds of teams × thousands of players = an asset store that can't be maintained, rendered efficiently, or updated without a full regeneration cycle. The right answer was one base PNG per sport — 3D jersey shape with depth and highlight baked in — plus an SVG colour layer on top. Every jersey looks unique. It's one asset with a colour swap. Wrote the full SVG generation pipeline in Claude Code: jersey_maker.py, svg_generator.py, jersey_skin_generator_v3.py.

Teams vs Players — an IA fix that looked like a design choice

Early versions showed teams and players in the same jersey grid. A KKR team jersey next to a Messi jersey next to a Dhoni jersey — same format, completely different relationship type.

The split: two visual languages for two types of fandom
Teams → shown as crests and flags in rounded cards. Players → shown as jerseys with name and number. The format signals the relationship type before the user reads the label. This isn't an aesthetic upgrade — it's an information architecture fix. The fact that it also looks better is secondary.

The Google AI Studio fan-pic prototype — used to validate visual direction before any engineering commitment

Where I overruled AI
During the brainstorming sessions, the AI kept pushing toward a gamification-heavy model — points, leaderboards, streaks on the profile itself. I rejected this framing. Gamification on a profile surface signals "we're trying to manipulate you." Fandom is intrinsic — it doesn't need points. The badges we designed unlock based on genuine behaviour (supporting MI 5 times, watching 3 WPs) not manufactured progression. The distinction matters to users even when they can't articulate why.

08 — Scoping for IPL

What shipped, what didn't, and one UX call forced by the backend

The deadline moved mid-project — we needed to ship before IPL. Most scope cuts were clean. One wasn't.

The moderation consolidation — a UX change driven by engineering
Face moderation and content moderation originally sat at two separate points in the avatar flow. Engineering needed them consolidated — two sequential moderation APIs created latency that made the 30-second generation time even worse. Collapsing them into one gate changed the UX: the error state now had to carry both failure modes clearly. "This photo didn't pass our guidelines" covers both cases, which is slightly less specific than I'd have liked. Trade-off accepted.

What we can say. What we can't.

Post-IPL completion and avatar generation rate
What I won't claim
I'm not attributing Watch Party retention or contest entry lift to this project. There's no clean causal line between "profile updated" and "user spent more in contests." Anyone who draws that line in a case study is bullshitting. The honest proxy metrics are: profile completion rate, avatar generation rate, and whether users who complete profiles behave differently in Watch Party. That data will exist in 8–12 weeks post-IPL.
What we were wrong about
We underestimated real-time AI generation at mobile scale. 30-second generation times, colour bleed, and cropping artifacts were real post-launch issues. The loading state buffer I'd designed helped — but I hadn't pushed hard enough on generation quality thresholds before shipping. I should have defined "acceptable quality" as a go/no-go criterion during design, not discovered the gap in production. That's on me, not engineering.

10 — The Takeaway

What this project actually was
I identified a broken product loop and built the layer that enables it to work.
Watch Party is a social product that couldn't behave like one because there was no identity substrate. This project doesn't fix Watch Party retention — it builds the infrastructure that makes fixing it possible. The profile is a side effect. The real output is structured fandom data: who supports which team, which players they love, which sport they care about. That data is what personalisation runs on. That personalisation is what tribal connections are built from. Those connections are what bring users back for the next match.

That's the chain. This project builds the first link.
